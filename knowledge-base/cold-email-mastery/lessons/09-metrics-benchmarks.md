## The only reply rate that means anything is the positive one

> "Reply rates: 20-30% vs. 2-3%."
> — Michel Lieben, 2026-06-23 · [source](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/michel-lieben_how-the-top-1-actually-run-cold-email-in-activity-7475138338602119169-DtWv)

That gap — **the top 1% run 20-30% reply rates while the average operator sits at 2-3%** — is the single most useful benchmark in this lesson. But a raw reply rate lumps "interested" in with "unsubscribe," "wrong person," and "stop emailing me." A 20% reply rate made of angry replies is a 0% campaign. So the number you actually steer on is the **positive reply rate**: replies that signal a real conversation. Everything below is about measuring that, not the vanity number on the dashboard.

## Stop measuring open rate — it actively costs you replies

> "adding the open tracking kills the reply rate because of that warning in the inbox."
> — OutboundPhD, 2026-07-01 · [source](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/outboundphd_take-this-as-your-sign-that-tracking-open-activity-7478138689835925505-odc7)

Open tracking is the most-worshipped and most-useless metric in cold email. The tracking pixel triggers Gmail and Outlook's "images are hidden, mark as spam?" warning, which **suppresses the reply rate** you're actually trying to grow. OutboundPhD watched the same campaign move on nothing but that toggle. The lesson: **turn open tracking off**, and never optimize toward a number that's costing you the outcome.

## What the operators actually track

> "Placement tests do NOT measure your real cold email deliverability."
> — Atishay (Hyperke), 2026-05-29 · [source](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/atishay-hyperke_having-sent-well-over-20m-cold-emails-im-activity-7466126228110897153--8VR)

> "What we actually track:"
> — Atishay (Hyperke), 2026-05-29 · [source](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/atishay-hyperke_having-sent-well-over-20m-cold-emails-im-activity-7466126228110897153--8VR)

Placement tests and warmup scores are lab conditions — they don't tell you whether real prospects are seeing you. After 20M+ sends, Atishay's live dashboard is four numbers: **reply rate, positive reply ratio, 550 bounce rate, and wrong-person reply percentage**. Notice what's missing — opens. Notice what's there — the **positive reply ratio** (quality of replies) and the **wrong-person percentage** (a direct read on whether your targeting is broken). These four are your health panel.

## The metric to optimize is positive response rate, on a weekly loop

> "I had the idea that instead of running a loop every 5 minutes to try to improve an outcome, we can run it every week on the data from cold email campaigns in order to increase positive response rates."
> — OutboundPhD, 2026-06-02 · [source](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/outboundphd_we-launched-a-fully-ai-managed-campaign-for-activity-7467595535072276483-2NmD)

This is the whole philosophy in one line: **pick positive response rate as your north-star metric, then improve it on a weekly cadence** — not a panicked daily one. Cold email data is too thin to read hour-to-hour; you need a week of volume before a change means anything. Optimize the positive number, review weekly, let the funnel accumulate signal.

## The funnel math: emails → replies → positive → calls → close

> "Again, this hinges on the fact that your ACV supports this math. Don't be sending out AirPods/gift cards for a $1,000 contract!"
> — Nick Abraham, 2026-06-01 · [source](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/nick-abraham_i-used-to-think-sales-reps-offering-airpods-activity-7467189120591659008-33R6)

Every stage multiplies. Sends × reply rate × positive-reply share × show rate × close rate = revenue — and **the whole model only works if your ACV can absorb the spend at the top**. Nick's point is that the funnel isn't a set of isolated metrics; it's a chain, and you must know your deal size before you decide how aggressively to fill the top. When the number at the bottom is wrong, walk it back up the chain to find the broken stage — that's how you diagnose.

## Diagnose by stage: which number is actually broken?

> "Company 2 is spending 5X on inboxes to get 20% of the meetings company A is generating."
> — Nikita (Maildoso), 2026-06-29 · [source](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/nikita-maildoso-2b6a6755_friendly-reminder-if-your-message-is-dogsh-activity-7477328064683659264-R5sh)

This is the most important diagnostic in the corpus. Two companies, same channel: one spends **5X the infrastructure to book 20% of the meetings**. The broken stage wasn't volume — it was the message and the single lazy campaign feeding every lead into one funnel. If your reply rate is fine but positive replies are low, the problem is fit/targeting. If sends are huge but meetings are flat, the problem is message quality, not inbox count. **More inboxes never fixes a bad message** — measure meetings-per-inbox, not raw sends.

## Sending volume is a formula, not a guess

> "the formula is dead simple:\ndaily volume = domains x inboxes per domain x emails per inbox"
> — Richard Illingworth, 2026-06-04 · [source](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/richard-illingworth_most-people-who-try-to-send-high-volume-cold-activity-7468329242548699136-2kRw)

> "once most people scale past 100 sends, they end up in spam within a WEEK."
> — Richard Illingworth, 2026-06-04 · [source](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/richard-illingworth_most-people-who-try-to-send-high-volume-cold-activity-7468329242548699136-2kRw)

Don't back into volume by guessing. **Daily volume = domains × inboxes per domain × emails per inbox** — build up to the number you need, don't force it out of too few inboxes. The failure mode is scaling raw sends per inbox: **past ~100 sends the naive way, you're in spam within a week**, and every downstream metric craters at once. Volume is an input you engineer, not a lever you crank.

## Warmup and coverage have their own benchmarks

> "we target a 60% reply rate during warmup."
> — Richard Illingworth, 2026-07-01 · [source](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/richard-illingworth_in-the-last-24-months-ive-warmed-up-360000-activity-7478142564441784321-Ko5S)

> "Stack them together? You're hitting 91%+ coverage."
> — Kenny Damian, 2026-06-01 · [source](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kenny-damian-90aba221a_we-hit-90-email-coverage-by-trusting-five-activity-7467209815841357824-g5lU)

Before a single cold send, two numbers should already be green. **Warmup reply rate targets 60%** — that's the internal signal your inboxes are trusted. And **email coverage should hit 91%+** by stacking multiple enrichment tools (one finder like Prospeo alone might only cover ~68% of your list). Low coverage silently shrinks your effective TAM; low warmup silently tanks deliverability. Both distort every funnel number after them, so verify them first.

## Your KPIs change as you scale

> "Your infra requirements change at every tier. So does your copy strategy, and so do the KPIs you should even be tracking."
> — Atishay (Hyperke), 2026-06-19 · [source](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/atishay-hyperke_weve-sent-30m-cold-emails-for-clients-targeting-activity-7473730849226518528-7ZzU)

A 100/day campaign and a 100K/day campaign are not the same game measured at different volumes — **the KPIs themselves change by tier**. At low volume you can read individual replies and optimize copy by hand; at high volume you're watching aggregate positive-reply rate and week-over-week drift. Don't import a benchmark built for one sending tier and apply it to another.

## Meetings math: measure show rate and speed-to-lead, not just booked

> "Call within 5-30 minutes. You want to catch them while your email is still top of mind."
> — Nick Abraham, 2026-06-08 · [source](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/nick-abraham_the-second-you-get-a-positive-reply-to-a-activity-7469796976767750145-Oogi)

> "This is one of the LOWEST-effort moves you can make to 2x your show rate."
> — Atishay (Hyperke), 2026-06-05 · [source](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/atishay-hyperke_this-is-one-of-the-lowest-effort-moves-you-activity-7468662954813460481-wNUl)

A booked meeting is not a held meeting, so **show rate is its own metric** — and it's cheap to move. Atishay **2x'd show rate** just by switching the calendar to 15-minute slots. Nick's lever is speed: **call a positive reply within 5-30 minutes**, while your email is still top of mind. If your booked-to-held ratio is leaking, the fix is almost never more reminder emails — it's speed-to-lead and a tighter offer.

## Key takeaway

Reply rate is the number everyone reports and the number that lies the most. The operators who scale measure the **positive reply rate** as their north star, track a four-metric health panel (reply, positive ratio, 550 bounce, wrong-person %), and read the funnel as a chain — sends → replies → positive → calls → held → close — so a broken bottom-line number gets diagnosed to a specific stage instead of "fixed" by adding inboxes. Benchmark yourself against the real numbers (20-30% reply for the elite, 60% warmup reply, 91%+ coverage, sub-5-30-min speed-to-lead), optimize on a weekly loop, and never chase a metric — like open rate — that actively costs you the outcome.

## Questions for you (Sophiya)

1. Bleed AI's proven engine runs ~13.4% reply rate but the leads are low-fit — which means the funnel is broken at the **positive-reply / wrong-person stage**, not the reply stage. Are we actually tracking positive-reply ratio and wrong-person-reply % as separate numbers, or is that healthy 13.4% masking a quality problem we'd catch instantly with Atishay's four-metric panel?
2. What's our real booked → held → closed math per campaign right now, and do we know our show rate as a distinct number — or are we only celebrating "meetings booked" and quietly losing them between booking and the call?
3. Given our ACV, does the incentive/gift-card math even pencil out for us the way it does for Nick's $50K-ACV clients — and if not, where does the funnel need to over-index instead (positive-reply quality vs. raw volume)?

## Questions you should be asking (that we haven't yet)

- What is the minimum sample size (sends/replies) before we're allowed to call a campaign "working" or "dead"? We keep panic-editing on three days of data when the corpus says give it two weeks.
- Which single stage of our funnel, if improved 20%, adds the most revenue — and are we spending optimization effort there, or on the stage that's easiest to tweak?

## Beyond the corpus — additional 2026 context

- Google and Microsoft's 2024 bulk-sender rules made **spam-complaint rate (keep it under ~0.1-0.3%) and authenticated SPF/DKIM/DMARC** hard gates, not nice-to-haves — a metric the corpus references indirectly via deliverability but worth tracking explicitly alongside the 550 bounce rate.
- With opens now unreliable (Apple Mail Privacy Protection inflates them, and tracking pixels hurt replies), the industry has largely moved to **reply-and-meeting-based attribution**; if any tooling still reports open rate as a KPI, treat it as noise, not signal.
