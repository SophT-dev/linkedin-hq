# Stage 4: Follow Up and Book the Call

**Here is the truth that most people building their own cold email system get wrong: the money is not in the first email. It is in the fourth.** We have watched thousands of sequences run, and the pattern holds every single time. If you stop after one send, you are voluntarily leaving nearly half your pipeline in the ground. This stage is about the boring, unglamorous discipline that separates a system that books calls from a system that just makes noise.

### Why one email is never enough

When we break down where replies actually come from, the split looks like this: roughly **58% of replies land on email 1, and about 42% come from the follow-ups.** Read that again. If you send once and walk away, you are throwing out more than 40% of the people who were willing to talk to you. They were not offended. They were busy, buried, or they meant to reply and forgot.

The reply curve by step, as a share of total replies, runs approximately:

- **Email 1:** ~35%
- **Email 2:** ~30%
- **Email 3:** ~20%
- **Email 4:** ~15%

*(directional: verify for your setup)*

Notice that email 2 nearly matches email 1. The person who "ignored" you the first time is often your most likely reply on the second. Persistence is not annoyance. Done right, it is a service.

### The default cadence

Our starting cadence is a **4-touch thread over roughly two weeks: day 0, day 3, day 7, and day 11.** Send in the *recipient's* timezone, not yours, and skip weekends so you land in a Monday-to-Friday work rhythm.

The workable range is **4 to 7 touches, spaced 3 to 4 days apart** *(directional: verify for your setup)*. Fewer than four and you leave replies on the table. More than seven and you cross from persistent into pest. Pick a number inside that band and hold it.

> 💡 The gap matters as much as the count. Too tight and you look automated and desperate. Too loose and they forget who you are between touches. Three to four days is the sweet spot.

### The 3-Offers rotation

The single biggest reason follow-ups fail is that they are the same email three times. The fix is to lead each touch with a **different value lens** so the thread reads like you genuinely had several distinct things to say. Rotate across four angles:

1. **Save Time**: the hours this hands back.
2. **Make Money**: the revenue or pipeline it opens.
3. **Save Money**: the cost or waste it removes.
4. **Mitigate Risk**: the downside it protects against.

Each follow-up leads with a fresh lens. Same outcome underneath, but a new door into it every time.

### Follow-ups must stand alone

Never send "just bumping this to the top of your inbox." That line tells the reader you have nothing new to offer, so please pay attention to the old thing. Every touch must carry its own reason to exist and be readable *without* the prior email. Here are three archetypes we lean on, with a fill-in template each.

**Archetype 1: Apologetic (bad-timing).** Lowers the pressure and gives them an easy on-ramp.

```
Subject: (blank, threaded reply)

I may have caught you at a bad time last week.

If [problem] isn't on your plate right now, no worries at all.
But if [specific outcome] would still be useful, I can [tiny next step].

Worth a look?
```

**Archetype 2: Context / founder story.** Adds a NEW reason to care by revealing why you are reaching out.

```
Subject: why I keep thinking about [company]

Quick context on why I reached out.

We built this after watching [specific situation] cost teams like
yours [cost/consequence]. When I saw [trigger about their company],
it clicked that you might hit the same wall.

Here's the one thing that changes it: [single concrete point].
```

**Archetype 3: Scarcity / breakup.** The last touch. Clean, no guilt, easy to reply to.

```
Subject: closing the loop

I don't want to keep landing in your inbox.

If [outcome] isn't a priority this quarter, I'll leave it here.
If it is, just reply "[one word]" and I'll send [next step].

Either way, thanks for reading.
```

### Threading mechanics

How you thread controls whether you even get seen:

- **Email 1:** a fresh, specific subject line.
- **Email 2:** a **blank-subject threaded reply** that stacks under email 1, so it reads as a natural bump inside the same conversation.
- **Email 3:** a **brand-new thread with a new subject.** This is deliberate. It escapes the "already ignored" bucket that a dead thread falls into and gives you a clean shot at the inbox again.

> 📌 A thread they have skimmed twice is a thread they will skim past a third time. Break the pattern by breaking the thread.

### The CTA ladder: match the ask to the temperature

The number-one killer of first emails is asking for too much too soon. On a cold first touch, a **soft, low-friction interest ask beats a direct meeting ask by roughly 3 to 1.** But once someone is warm, a **specific time ask converts about 2.5x better than a soft one.** The ask has to move as the relationship moves.

A raw Calendly link in email 1 is dead on arrival. You are asking a stranger to put time on their calendar before they have decided they care.

Think of your asks as a **ladder** you climb as trust grows:

1. **Cliffhanger**: tease something they have to reply to unlock.
2. **Value**: offer a specific, useful thing.
3. **Interest**: "worth a look?" low-commitment yes.
4. **Confirmation**: "want me to send it over?"
5. **Direct**: a specific time to talk.
6. **Deflection**: "if not you, who owns this?"

Cold touches live near the top of the ladder. You only reach for the direct time ask once they have signaled warmth.

### Booking and lead magnets

If your path to the call runs through a resource, follow these rules:

- **Never attach anything in email 1.** An unrequested attachment from a stranger is a spam signal and a delete trigger.
- **Ask permission first.** "Want me to send it over?" A yes is a micro-commitment that pulls them deeper.
- **Deliver within 24 hours** of the yes, while the intent is hot.
- **Validate the magnet TITLE before you build the whole thing.** Run the title as an offer in a short campaign. If the title alone does not earn replies, the asset behind it will not save you. Never build a big deliverable on an unproven promise.
- **Keep a straight line to cash.** Every magnet should hand off naturally into a conversation about working together, not dead-end as a free giveaway.

### On a positive reply, act immediately

This is where most self-built systems bleed. A positive reply comes in, and the operator drafts a thoughtful email and waits. That is a mistake. **Do not reply-and-wait. Look them up and reach out across channels within minutes.**

Speed and multichannel on the same fresh signal move the numbers hard:

- **Email plus a call:** ~2.5x lift on positive replies.
- **Email plus LinkedIn:** ~1.9x lift.

*(directional: verify for your setup)*

A short, personal **voice note of 25 to 30 seconds** that references something specific about them converts well. It proves a human is on the other end, and it is far harder to ignore than one more line of text.

> ⚠️ The window on a hot reply is measured in minutes, not days. The person who was excited about your idea this morning has moved on to three fires by this afternoon.

### The Day-21 decision

Do not marry a campaign. Run it, then judge it against hard thresholds at the three-week mark:

- **Positive reply rate ≥ 1%:** viable: keep it running.
- **Positive reply rate ≥ 2%:** scale candidate: pour more volume in.
- **Positive reply rate < 0.5%:** kill it and rebuild from the copy up.

*(directional: verify for your setup)*

No sentiment, no "let's give it another week." The number decides.

Build the whole thread before you send the first line. A follow-up you write in a hurry on day 3 is a follow-up that says "just bumping this." Plan the persistence, and the pipeline follows.
