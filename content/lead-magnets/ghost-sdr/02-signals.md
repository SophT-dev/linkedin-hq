# Stage 1: Find Leads at the Right Time (Signals)

**Most cold email fails before a single word is written.** The problem is not the subject line or the CTA. It is that the email lands on someone who has no reason to care today. Stage 1 fixes that at the root. We build every campaign on **signals**: specific, findable facts that give a prospect a real reason to hear from you *right now*. Get this stage right and the rest of the system gets easier, because you are talking to fewer people who actually want the conversation.

### Why signals beat volume

A signal is not a personality trait or a job title. It is an event or a condition you can point to. "VP of Sales, 200-person SaaS" is a filter. "Just posted three sales roles this week" is a signal. The difference matters because a signal does one job extremely well: it *shrinks your list to the people with a reason to buy*.

That is the whole mechanism. Signals do not make weak copy convert. They do not rescue a vague offer. What they do is change the denominator. Instead of emailing 10,000 plausible people, you email 400 people who just did something that maps to your offer. Everything downstream gets cheaper and warmer.

> 💡 The mental model: signals shrink volume to the *right* people. They are a targeting tool, not a copy tool.

### The 3 laws of signals

1. **A signal shrinks your list, it does not fix your copy.** If your offer is unclear, a perfect signal just gets you a polite no from the right person. Fix the offer separately.
2. **A signal you do not reference in the email is wasted.** The entire value of a signal is that it lets you open with something true and specific. If the email could have been sent to anyone, the signal did nothing. You paid to find it, so *use it in the first two lines*.
3. **Any one-click, single-database signal is already dead.** If you can pull it from one tool with one filter, so can everyone else, and the prospect has already seen ten emails using it. The move is to **stack 2 or more signals**, or **generate your own** from raw public sources. Uniqueness is the point.

### Match signal freshness to the source

Not all signals age the same way, and the source you pull from decides how fresh they are.

Large aggregated databases are slow. They are excellent for stable "life-stage" facts (company size, industry, tech category), but those facts resolve **6 to 12 months late**. If you need genuinely fresh intent, under roughly **90 days**, you have to go to the source where the event actually happens.

- **Slow, stable signals** live in big databases. Good for segmentation, not for timing.
- **Fresh, event-native signals** live in public, real-time sources: product launch directories, job boards, funding filings, news feeds, maps and review sites, and public social posts.

> 📌 Rule of thumb: if the signal is time-sensitive, do not trust a database to have it fresh. Go to the event-native public source and read it yourself.

### A menu of high-intent triggers

Here are ten trigger types that consistently map to a reason to buy. For each, the *category* of public source where it lives (find the specific tool yourself):

1. **Executive or leadership hire**: press releases, company news pages, public profile changes.
2. **Funding round**: public funding filings and startup/funding news feeds.
3. **Active hiring for a specific role**: job boards and company careers pages.
4. **Tech-stack install or removal**: public site-technology lookups.
5. **Web-traffic spike**: public traffic-estimate sources.
6. **Publicly posting about the problem you solve**: social platforms and community forums.
7. **Competitor churn or review complaints**: review sites and public ratings.
8. **First-time vs repeat founder**: public company registries and founder history.
9. **Geographic or expansion move**: news, job locations, new-office announcements.
10. **Award or press mention**: industry awards lists and press coverage.

### Trigger decay: always be finding the next one

Every good trigger wears out. The pattern is predictable. First-name personalization worked, then everyone did it. Funding triggers worked, then every tool shipped a funding filter. Job-change triggers followed. Social-post triggers are being commoditized right now.

The lesson is not "use trigger X." It is that *the edge is temporary by design*. Whatever converts today is being copied. Build the habit of hunting the next under-used signal before your current one saturates.

### Second-order thinking

When a big announcement drops, everyone piles in with the obvious message. A company raises a round and gets fifty emails that all say "congrats on the raise." That is first-order, and the prospect deletes all fifty.

Instead, react to the *consequence* of the news, not the news itself. A raise means hiring pressure, new targets, scaling pains. A leadership hire means a mandate to change something in the first 90 days. Speak to the thing the event *causes*, and you are the one email in the pile that read the situation instead of the headline.

### The numbers that keep you honest

- **Sourcing cost** anchors around **$5 to $12 per 1,000 leads** *(directional: verify for your setup)*. If you are paying wildly more to simply find contacts, something is off.
- **Personalization is now binary.** First-name and company tokens alone land near **3% reply**; genuine signal-and-context relevance lands **15 to 30%** *(directional: verify for your setup)*. This is the token-vs-signal gap. Merge tags are table stakes, not personalization. Real relevance is a different tier of result.
- **The scale diagnostic:** if you contact **under ~500** prospects to earn one positive reply, you are in healthy scale territory. If it takes **over ~1,000**, something is broken, usually the targeting or the offer, not the copy.

> ⚠️ Do not read the token-vs-signal gap as a "spend more on writing" instruction. The gap is about *relevance*, and relevance is decided at the signal stage, before you write anything.
