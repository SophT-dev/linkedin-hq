## 1. Why most lists fail (fit is not intent)

Almost every cold email list is built the same way. You pick a company size, an industry, and a job title, you pull a few thousand contacts that match, and you start sending. On paper it looks like a great list. Every name fits your ideal customer. And then the replies never come, and you blame the copy.

The copy is almost never the real problem. Reply rates die at the list stage, not the writing stage. You can write the best email of your life, but if the person reading it has no reason to care this week, it lands nowhere.

The reason is a mix-up between two things that feel the same but aren't:

- **Fit** is whether a company *looks like* your customer. Right size, right industry, right role. It's a yes or no.
- **Intent** is whether they're showing signs they might actually buy *right now*. It moves up and down over time.

Most lists are 100% fit and 0% intent. A company that fits your ICP (your ideal customer profile, the type of business you sell to best) but has no reason to think about your problem today is still a cold, cold email. You're a good match talking to someone who isn't listening yet.

The whole game is finding the small slice of your fit-list that also has a live reason to care, and reaching them while that reason is still fresh. That slice is the "right person" this playbook is about.

## 2. The 6 buying signals that actually matter

A buying signal is any real event that tells you a company just got more likely to want what you sell. Not every signal is worth chasing. These six are the ones that consistently earn replies for outbound.

1. **Job changes.** Someone new steps into a role you sell to, a new head of sales, a new founder-hire running growth. In their first 90 days they want a fast win and they're open to tools the last person never tried. This is the single warmest signal in B2B.

2. **Funding.** A company just raised a round. They now have budget and real pressure to grow fast, which is exactly the moment an outbound system becomes worth paying for. The round is public, dated, and easy to tie a message to.

3. **Hiring.** A company posting for SDRs, account executives, or a demand-gen lead is telling you, out loud, that they're investing in exactly the area you help with. You don't have to guess their priorities. The job post is the priority.

4. **Tech adoption.** They just added a tool that shows where their head is, a CRM, an email platform, an outbound tool. Someone who just bought Instantly or Clay is thinking about outbound today in a way they weren't last quarter.

5. **Content engagement.** Someone who liked or commented on a post about your topic raised their hand in public. They told the room they care about this. A comment on a cold-email post is a warmer starting point than any title filter.

6. **Competitor dissatisfaction.** A company using a competitor, and grumbling about it, is pre-educated and pre-unhappy demand. They already know they need the thing you sell, and they're already unsatisfied. Win them with stronger proof, never with a callout. Nobody likes being told their current tool is bad.

Notice what all six have in common. None of them is a job title. Every one is a dated event you can point to and say "this is why I'm reaching out today."

## 3. How to score and stack signals

Once you're collecting signals, you need a way to rank accounts so you work the hottest ones first. Three simple rules do most of the work.

**Keep fit and intent apart.** Store the yes/no fit check separately from the intent score. Never blend them into one number. If you fuse them, a campaign that flops leaves you unable to tell whether you targeted the wrong companies or reached the right ones at the wrong time. Two dials, read separately.

**Let signals decay.** A signal is only as good as it is fresh. A funding round from eight months ago is old news; everyone already pitched them. A rough rule that works: a signal loses about half its weight every month. Anything past 60 to 90 days is close to cold again. Score recent events high and let old ones fade on their own.

**Reward stacking of different types.** Three *different kinds* of signal on one account beat ten of the same kind. A company that just raised a round, hired a new head of sales, and posted three SDR openings is practically standing on a chair waving at you. Give each fresh signal a point or two, add a bonus when different signal types show up together, and knock points off for age. Anything over your threshold gets worked first.

That's the entire system. Fit is the gate. Intent is a decaying, stacked score. The accounts at the top of that list are the ones worth your best email this week.

## 4. Finding the right person at the right account

A hot account isn't a person. You still have to pick the one human to email, and this is where most teams either guess a title or blast the whole company.

Title filters are a starting point, not the answer. The better question is simple: **who owns the pain?** Who at this company loses sleep, or loses their job, if this problem stays unsolved? That's the person who will actually read your email and act on it.

For outbound help, the pain-owner is usually the founder at a small company, a VP of Sales at a bigger one, sometimes a head of growth. It's almost never the SDR (no budget, no authority) and almost never the CEO of a 500-person company (too far from the day-to-day to feel it). Map the pain to the person, then reach that one person.

And it really is one person. Emailing five people at the same company in the same week looks like spam, splits the reply across inboxes, and makes you look like you didn't do your homework. One right person, one strong reason, beats five sprayed guesses every time.

## 5. The per-account "why now" line

This is the line that carries the whole email. One sentence, written fresh for each account, that says why you're reaching out to *this* company *this* week.

"Saw you just brought on three SDRs." "Noticed you closed your Series A last month." "You commented on Michel's post about signal-based sending, so this is probably on your mind."

That single line answers the only real question in the reader's head: why me, and why now. It ties your outreach to a real event they recognize, which is the difference between a relevant note from someone who paid attention and one more mail merge in a full inbox.

Two rules make the "why now" line land:

- **Keep the first message a 60-second yes, not a call ask.** Offer something small and useful right away, a quick teardown, a benchmark against their peers, one specific idea. "Book a 30-minute demo" asks for a lot from someone who just met you. A tiny helpful thing asks for almost nothing.
- **Lead with proof, never a callout.** Especially with the competitor-dissatisfaction signal, don't tell them their tool is bad. Show them a better result and let them do the math.

Write the "why now" line first, before the rest of the email. If you can't write a true one for an account, that account isn't ready, and no amount of clever copy will fix it. That's the honest filter this whole playbook comes down to.

## 6. The point of all this

Volume outbound is dying for a plain reason. A mailbox can only send 20 to 30 real emails a day before deliverability (the thing that decides whether your email lands in the inbox or in spam) starts to break. Sending more doesn't get you more replies, it just burns your domains faster. The edge left in 2026 isn't more sends. It's better-chosen ones.
