## First, what a "skill" is here

A skill is not a feature you buy. It is a plain text file that holds a process you already do in your head, written down once so Claude runs it the same way every time. You describe how you build a list or write a first line, you save that description, and now it is repeatable.

Cold email goes wrong in the same places for almost everyone: a soft list, random timing, a robotic first line, follow-ups that just say "bumping this," and no record of what worked. The ten skills below are one part each of a real pipeline, patterns working operators run every day, in the version you can build this afternoon from a text file and a clear description of how you already work.

## The 10 skills

### 1. The ICP Definer

**What it does:** Turns a fuzzy "we sell to B2B founders" into a tight, filterable profile: exact titles, company size, the trigger that means they need you now, and the disqualifiers that rule the rest out.

**The outcome:** Every later step gets sharper, because they all read this file first. Personalization in 2026 is close to binary: first-name and company tokens do almost nothing, around a 3% reply, while real business-context relevance jumps to 15-30%. A tight ICP is what makes that relevance possible.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Tell Claude, "Interview me about who we actually close, then write a one-page profile: titles, company size, the buying trigger, and three hard disqualifiers." Answer using your last ten won deals, not your dream client. Save it. Every other skill points at it.

### 2. The List Auditor

**What it does:** Reads a raw list and scores each row against the ICP file before you spend a cent on verification. It flags rows that miss the profile, guesses at catch-alls, and separates maybes from clean fits.

**The outcome:** You send to a list you trust instead of a pile you hope is fine. This is where bounce rate gets decided. A list that never gets cleaned can run 6.5% bounce or higher, which tells inbox providers you are sending cold and buries even your good emails.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Say, "When I paste a list, score each contact against my ICP file from 1 to 5, flag any role account or catch-all, and split the output into keep, check, and drop." Save it, and run every raw list through it before verification.

### 3. The Account Researcher

**What it does:** Takes one company and writes back a short brief: what they sell, where they are likely stuck in your area, and one specific detail worth opening with.

**The outcome:** You reach out sounding like you did your homework, because you did, the difference between "I help companies like yours" and a first line that names the exact thing they just changed.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Tell Claude, "Given a company name and website, give me a short brief: what they do, their likely pain in my area, and one specific hook." Save it, and run it on accounts worth a real message, not a template.

### 4. The "Why Now" Finder

**What it does:** Looks for the timing signal that makes this the right week to reach out: a funding round, a fresh hire in a relevant seat, a tech switch, a job posting that hints at the pain you fix.

**The outcome:** You email people when something just changed, which is when they are open to a change. Michel Lieben's rule is to only email buyers who already want you, using real signals instead of a cold static list. A trigger turns a name into a reason to write.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Say, "For this account, look for recent public signals: funding, key hires, hiring posts, tech changes. Tell me the strongest one and what it probably means for them." Save it. Feed it the accounts your List Auditor kept.

### 5. The Opener Writer

**What it does:** Writes the first line and only the first line, grounded in the trigger the "Why Now" Finder surfaced, with hard rules against the phrases that get an email deleted.

**The outcome:** Your opener earns the second line instead of getting archived. Bake the rules in: never "I hope this finds you well," never a naked "I saw you raised a Series A" (inbox filters pattern-match that instantly now). The move that works is signal, then implication, then proof. "Saw you opened a second location. For home-services teams we work with, that usually means follow-up on new leads slips. Here is what fixed it." The data point is not the hook. The implication is.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Give Claude your banned-phrase list and two openers you are proud of. Say, "Write three first lines in this style. Always go signal, then what it implies, then a one-line proof. Never open with a greeting or a raw compliment." Save it, then edit the first few by hand until it learns your ear.

### 6. The Sequence Builder

**What it does:** Designs the follow-ups, not as "just checking in" bumps, but as a short sequence where each touch adds a new angle or a new proof point.

**The outcome:** You catch the replies the first email missed. Around 58% of replies come from email one, but 42% come from follow-ups, so quitting after one send leaves almost half your results behind. The sweet spot is 4 to 7 touches, 3 to 4 days apart, each carrying something new.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Tell Claude, "Given my first email, write a 4-touch sequence spaced 3 to 4 days apart. Each follow-up adds a new angle, proof point, or softer ask, never just 'bumping this.' Keep each under 75 words." Save it, and pair it with the Opener Writer for a campaign's first draft.

### 7. The Deliverability Sentinel

**What it does:** Runs the pre-send checklist and watches inbox health while a campaign runs. It checks warmup is done, per-inbox volume stays sane, the copy is plain, and bounce has not crept into the danger zone.

**The outcome:** Your emails keep landing instead of sliding to spam. The rules are the consensus ones: never send cold volume from your main domain, warm every new inbox 2 to 4 weeks and keep warming it after, hold each inbox to 20 to 25 sends a day and scale by adding inboxes rather than raising that ceiling, and keep the first touch plain text with no links. Treat bounce as an alarm: many operators auto-pause the moment it crosses 2%, and anything past 5% is a reputation emergency, stop and clean first.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Say, "Before I launch, check this campaign against my rules: warmup done, under 25 sends per inbox a day, plain text, no first-touch links, list verified. Then each day, ask me the bounce rate and tell me to pause if it crosses 2%." Save it as the gate every campaign passes before launch.

### 8. The Reply Triager

**What it does:** Sorts your replies into interested, not now, wrong person, and objection, then drafts a warm response for the ones worth your time. It shows you everything before a single message sends.

**The outcome:** An hour of inbox triage takes a few minutes, and you still hold the send button. The confirm gate is the load-bearing part, the thing that stops a wrong message reaching a real prospect.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Tell Claude, "Sort each reply into interested, not now, wrong person, or objection. For the interested and objection ones, draft a short warm reply in my voice. Never send. Show me the batch and wait." Save it, and run it each morning.

### 9. The Campaign Scoreboard

**What it does:** Pulls the numbers that matter and tells you plainly whether a campaign is working or just busy. It ignores open rate on purpose and scores on positive replies.

**The outcome:** You kill dead campaigns fast and pour more into the live ones, on facts instead of a gut feeling. Open tracking is close to useless in 2026 and can even hurt placement, so leave it out. The real benchmarks: reply rate averages 1 to 5%, good is 10% or more. Positive reply rate averages 0.1 to 0.5%, and 1 to 3% is good. Those two numbers are the truth.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Say, "Every week, ask me for sends, total replies, and positive replies per campaign. Give me reply rate and positive reply rate, compare to last week, and tell me plainly which to cut and which to feed." Save it, run it every Friday.

### 10. The Win Logger

**What it does:** Captures a proof point the moment it lands, a booked call, a strong reply, a closed deal, and files it with a note, so your best material is saved, not scrolled past.

**The outcome:** You build a proof library that writes half your future outreach for you. The Opener Writer and the Sequence Builder both get sharper pulling a real result instead of a vague claim. A logged win today is next month's best first line.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Tell Claude, "When I drop a screenshot or a result, save it, tag it by campaign and industry, and pull out the one specific stat I could use as proof later." Decide up front what counts as a win so the library stays sharp.

## How the ten become one pipeline

On their own, these are ten helpers. Chained, they are one loop you run every week.

The ICP Definer sets the target. The List Auditor cleans the names against it. The Account Researcher and the "Why Now" Finder turn the survivors into real reasons to reach out. The Opener Writer and the Sequence Builder turn those reasons into a campaign. The Deliverability Sentinel makes sure it lands, then watches it run. The Reply Triager handles what comes back. The Campaign Scoreboard says what to keep and what to cut. And the Win Logger feeds the wins back to the top, so the next round of openers is built on proof, not guesses.

That is the whole system, and each part reads the ICP file first, so the pipeline stays pointed at the same person. Build one this week, the ICP Definer or the Deliverability Sentinel are the ones that make the rest worth building, then add the next once the first has earned its place. A cold email operating system is not a weekend project. It is a handful of files that get smarter every time you run a campaign.
