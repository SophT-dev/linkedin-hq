# 🎯 8. What Kills Your Meeting Rate

A reply is not a booked call. After running millions of outbound emails, Eric laid out the exact patterns that quietly kill meeting rates — and they're mostly about the CTA and the follow-up, not the first email. Here are his six, in his words.

> After running millions of outbound emails, these are the patterns I see that kill meeting rates.

### 1. Using a calendar link as your CTA

> Calendar links feel convenient, but security filters + human behavior both work against them. People are more likely to reply "Sure, let's chat" than click a random link. Earn the reply first, then offer booking options.

The fix is sequencing: get the *reply* first, then hand over the calendar — don't lead with the link.

### 2. Not following up fast enough

> Speed is leverage. The longer you wait, the colder the lead gets. Reply the same day if you can — it signals you're paying attention.

### 3. Not following up enough

This is the pattern people underestimate most, and Eric tells on himself to prove it:

> There's a balance: don't spam, but if someone raised their hand once, don't stop until they book or say no. I positively responded to Rippling and it took that poor soul 8 follow ups for me to book. I'm still sorry but it wasn't my biggest priority.

The lesson: even a genuinely interested prospect (Eric himself) needed **8 follow-ups** to actually get on the calendar. Interest is not a booking.

> 📌 Proof: Eric's own words — "I positively responded to Rippling and it took that poor soul **8 follow ups** for me to book." A warm, hand-raised lead still needed eight touches; the rep who quit at three or four would have lost the meeting entirely.

### 4. Not adding value in follow-ups

Empty "checking in" touches waste the lead:

> Repeating "just checking in" is lazy. Each touch should reinforce why the call matters: ROI, risk avoidance, case study, new idea. After a few tries, you can even add a kicker (we've seen success offering a gift card to qualified prospects at this stage).

### 5. Not giving booking options

Don't force one path — offer both a specific time *and* the calendar link:

> Don't force a link or a back-and-forth. Do both.
> "Would Tuesday at 9am ET or Thursday at 2pm ET work? Or if it's easier, here's my calendar."

His reasoning covers both personality types: "Some people like booking with a calendly, others are offended by it. I don't get it but appease both people." You're "reducing friction while respecting preferences."

### 6. Not following up across channels

> A reply rate isn't the same as a meeting rate. Sometimes an extra LinkedIn DM or quick phone call takes a warm response over the finish line. People book with people, not just with inboxes.

### The bigger idea: give them something worth replying to

The reason follow-ups so often fall flat is they're generic. Eric's "creative ideas campaign" is his antidote — instead of an AI-generated slop compliment, you analyze the prospect's site and offer real, tangible ideas:

> This way we aren't sending AI generated slop compliments but instead, real tangible ways that we can help based on the company and contact's context.

He notes even a one-line version of this lifted results hard: "we also helped **3x the reply rates for John Thomas campaigns** by just adding this one line to his best templates."

Eric closes the meeting-rate post with the honest framing that ties it together:

> Just a couple of things we've seen make a difference that are obvious but sometimes we needed to be reminded more than we need to learn.

> 💡 Meetings die in the gap between reply and booking — earn the reply first, follow up fast, follow up *enough* (8 times if that's what it takes), make every touch add value, and always offer both a specific time and the calendar.
