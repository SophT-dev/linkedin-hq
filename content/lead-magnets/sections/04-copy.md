# ✍️ 4. The Copy Injection Formula

Eric's copywriting philosophy is not about writing more. It's about **injecting one researched line** into an otherwise clean email, and knowing the exact moments when you should skip personalization entirely. Here is how he turns research into copy that lands.

### Kill the generic AI first line

Eric ran the tests and reversed a position he used to preach. He no longer opens with an AI-written line scraped from a company's homepage:

> "The tests are in and we are not allowing generic AI personalizations based on company descriptions as first lines anymore. I could prove this raised reply rates but recently, in most markets, it falls flat."

The problem is that **sophisticated buyers recognize it instantly.** His own bad example is the tell: *"I saw you help fitness coaches retain clients and I wanted to get connected."* Those lines read like *"I noticed you're focused on helping local businesses..."* — the prospect has "seen it a thousand times." His rule now:

- **If you have a real signal** — something specific, custom-scraped, tied to their problem — use it.
- **If you don't have a signal**, do not fake one.

### Treat cold email like a banner ad

When there's no signal, Eric flips the whole strategy. Instead of forcing personalization, he sells the offer up front:

> "**If we don't have a signal:** We treat cold email like a banner ad. Put the offer in the preview text. Make it punchy. No fake personalization."

His reasoning: when someone scrolls on their phone, "they see the subject line + preview text together. That's your click opportunity." He weighs **preview text** even more heavily than subject lines — "More and more, I feel like preview text is more important than subject lines" — building on Will Allred's "Internal Camo" idea of writing a subject that looks like it came from a colleague, now extended into the preview line too (without lying).

And not every email needs a heavy build. In one campaign he got traction "with only 1 AI snippet," focused on a preview-text question relevant enough that "if it wasn't they could just ignore us," plus Oren Klaff's point about using **the buyer's own language** so they know you understand the problem.

### Inject the case study — often as a P.S.

Eric's favorite injection is a **case study pulled from the prospect's own website**, dropped in as a relevance hook or a P.S. line. Using Claygent (Clay's AI research agent) with 4o mini, he researches the case study for "under .01 per prospective company," then writes the injected line for "less than .001." His actual phrasings:

> "P.S. saw how you helped Intercom increase their email deliverability. Would be a great thing to promote in a newsletter shout out with us."

Or as a relevance opener: *"Hey {{first_name}} — saw that case study on the site with Intercom..."*

> 📌 Proof: Eric split-tested the case-study injection against the plain version and reported that "a couple times we have launched a validation test split testing the case study AI being included and not and every time so far, the copy mentioning the case studies did better." The research cost under $0.01 per company.

### Cite your sources

One hard-won rule: whenever you inject a data point, **name where it came from.** Similarweb once fed him wrong traffic numbers and prospects replied *"Was the point of this email to have your research be so wrong...?"* His fix — phrasings like *"Glassdoor told me your employees rated your company 4.7 stars"* or *"I got a notification from Crunchbase that you recently raised money."*

> "Every data point you bring into a campaign increases the odds you might be wrong. By citing your sources, the prospect sees you did research instead of thinking you're completely incorrect."

> 💡 One researched line, cited and specific — or no personalization at all and a punchy offer in the preview text. Never a fake AI first line.
