## First, what a "skill" actually is

A Claude skill is not a feature you buy. It is a plain text file, usually named `SKILL.md`, that holds a process you already do in your head, written down once so Claude can run it the same way every time. That's the whole trick. You describe how you do a thing, save the description, and now it's repeatable.

The reason this matters for a founder is simple. You are the bottleneck on maybe twenty small jobs a week. Writing the proposal. Prepping for the call. Categorizing the expenses. Reminding yourself what actually matters today. None of those need a full-time hire. They need a written-down process and something to run it. That's what the ten below are.

None of these are theory. Each one is a pattern working operators use every day, distilled to the version any founder can build in an afternoon with nothing but a text file and a clear description of how you already work. Let's go.

## The 10 skills

### 1. The Morning Coach

**What it does:** Runs a five-minute morning planning session. It reads yesterday's notes, looks at what's due, and tells you the two or three things that actually move money today, not the twenty that feel urgent.

**The outcome:** You stop starting the day in your inbox reacting to other people's priorities. You start it with a plan you can defend. Ten minutes of drift saved every morning is roughly an hour a week back.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Open Claude and say, "I want a morning planning skill. Each morning ask me what I got done, then look at my goals and my calendar, then give me the top three priorities and push back if I'm avoiding the hard one." Ask it to save that as a skill. Now one command runs your exact routine.

### 2. The Proposal Writer

**What it does:** Turns a job post or a discovery-call note into a tailored proposal in your voice, using your real past wins as proof, instead of a blank page and a sinking feeling.

**The outcome:** A proposal that used to eat forty minutes now takes five to review. More of them go out, and they sound like you, not like a template everyone else is also sending.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Paste in two proposals you're proud of. Tell Claude, "Learn my structure and tone from these. When I give you a new job post, write one the same way, and always pull a relevant win from my track record." Save it. Feed it a real post and edit the first draft until it's yours.

### 3. The Prospect Brief

**What it does:** Researches a company and the person you're about to talk to, then hands you a one-page brief: what they do, where they're likely stuck, and two things worth mentioning on the call.

**The outcome:** You walk into every sales conversation sounding like you did your homework, because you did. That is the difference between "tell me about your business" and "I noticed you just opened a second location, how's hiring going."

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Tell Claude, "When I give you a company name and a website, give me a short brief: what they sell, their likely pain in my service area, and two specific hooks I can open a call with." Save it. Run it before your next meeting instead of skimming their homepage in the parking lot.

### 4. The Meeting Prep

**What it does:** Takes the prospect brief and turns it into the actual conversation: an agenda, the three questions that surface budget and urgency, and the objections you should have answers ready for.

**The outcome:** You lead the call instead of following it. Prepared founders close more, not because they talk more, but because they ask better questions and don't get caught flat by the same three objections every time.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Say, "Given a prospect brief, build me a call plan: an agenda, discovery questions that uncover budget and timeline, and the likely objections with my best response to each." Save it. Pair it with skill three and you have a research-to-ready pipeline for every meeting.

### 5. The Weekly Scoreboard

**What it does:** Pulls your scattered numbers, calls booked, replies, cash in, content posted, into one plain weekly summary that tells you if the week was good or a lie you told yourself.

**The outcome:** You run on facts instead of vibes. Most founders feel busy and assume that means progress. A weekly scoreboard makes the gap between motion and results impossible to ignore, which is exactly what you need.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Decide the five numbers that actually matter for your business. Tell Claude, "Every Friday, ask me for these five numbers, compare them to last week, and tell me plainly what's working and what's slipping." Save it. The point is not a pretty dashboard, it's an honest one.

### 6. The Bookkeeper

**What it does:** Reads a messy export of transactions, sorts each one into the right category, and flags anything it isn't sure about instead of guessing. Month-end goes from a dreaded afternoon to a ten-minute review.

**The outcome:** Your books stay current without you learning to love spreadsheets. Come tax time, the sorting is already done and the only things left are the handful it flagged for you.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Give Claude your category list and a sample of past transactions so it learns your patterns. Say, "Categorize new transactions like these, and flag anything ambiguous rather than guessing." Save it. Run each export through it and only touch the flags.

### 7. The Repurposer

**What it does:** Takes one thing you already made, a call recording, a long post, a client win, and turns it into a week of shorter content in your voice, so you're not writing from scratch every day.

**The outcome:** You show up consistently without content becoming a second job. One good idea becomes five posts instead of one, and the consistency is what actually grows an audience, not any single viral hit.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Paste in a post that did well. Tell Claude, "This is my voice. When I give you a raw idea or a transcript, turn it into three short posts that sound like this, no marketing-speak, no fake enthusiasm." Save it. Now every idea has a multiplier attached to it.

### 8. The Competitor Watch

**What it does:** Reads a competitor's site, pricing page, or recent posts and tells you what changed and what it means for you, so you're not the last to know they dropped a new offer.

**The outcome:** You make positioning decisions with current information instead of a mental snapshot from six months ago. You spot the gap they're leaving open and the move they just copied from you.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Say, "When I paste a competitor's page or posts, summarize their offer and positioning, then tell me one thing they do better and one gap I can own." Save it. Run it monthly on your top two competitors and keep the notes.

### 9. The SOP Writer

**What it does:** Watches you describe a task once, then writes it up as a clean standard operating procedure a new hire could actually follow, so the thing living only in your head becomes something you can hand off.

**The outcome:** Delegation stops being scary. The reason founders won't let go of small tasks is that explaining them feels slower than doing them. This makes the explanation a one-time cost, and then it's off your plate for good.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Pick a task you do that someone else should. Tell Claude, "I'm going to walk you through how I do this. Turn it into a step-by-step SOP a new teammate could follow with no other context." Talk it through. Save the result and send it to whoever's taking it over.

### 10. The Second Brain

**What it does:** This is the one that makes the other nine sharp. It's a set of plain files, your company facts, your voice, your offers, your real numbers, that every other skill reads before it does its job. A second brain is not a fancy tool. It is markdown files in folders, referenced by name.

**The outcome:** Every skill above gets better because it stops guessing about your business and starts reading the truth. The proposal writer pulls your real wins. The repurposer uses your real voice. You write a fact down once and everything you own gets smarter.

**Build it in 15 minutes:** Make a folder. Write four short files: what your company does, who you sell to, how you sound, and your current numbers. Tell each of the other skills to read these first. That's the entire system. The magic was never the tool, it was writing your context down where it can be reused.

## The one thing that makes any of this stick

Here is the honest part, and it's the reason most people try this and quietly give up after a week.

A skill you copied off the internet, with none of your context in it, is a template. And templates get deleted, because generic output is worse than no output. The skills above only work because they carry your proposals, your voice, your numbers, your way of running a call. Strip that out and you're left with a fancy autocomplete that sounds like everyone else.

So build them light, then feed them your real stuff. Start with one. The Morning Coach or the Second Brain are the two that make the rest worth building. Get one running this week, use it every day, and add the next only when the first has earned its place. A founder OS is not a weekend project you finish. It's a handful of files that get a little smarter every time you use them.
