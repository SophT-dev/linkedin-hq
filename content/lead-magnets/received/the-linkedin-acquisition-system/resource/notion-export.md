### The Content-First Problem

The advice everyone got was: post consistently, build an audience, wait for inbound.

The logic sounds fine. It falls apart on one assumption, which is that your buyers are somewhere in the audience you accumulate. They usually aren't. You attract peers, competitors, and people who like your writing. Almost none of them are in-market for a five-figure engagement this quarter.

So the founder posts for eighteen months, becomes moderately well known, and still can't tell you where next month's revenue comes from.

Here's the reversal.

| Content-first | System-first |
| --- | --- |
| Post, then hope the right person sees it | Identify people already in-market, then engage them directly |
| Audience is whoever shows up | Audience is defined by buying behavior |
| Pipeline depends on you posting | Pipeline runs whether you post or not |
| Personal brand is the asset | The system is the asset, and it's transferable |

That last row is the one that matters most and it's the one nobody talks about.

A personal brand cannot be sold. If your revenue exists because you are known, then you are the business, and there is nothing for a buyer to purchase. A documented acquisition system that runs without you is an asset. That's the difference between a well-paid job and a company worth selling.

Content still has a place. It just isn't first. It's what makes the system convert better once the system exists.

By the end of this section you'll have three named audiences who already understand your problem, already believe a fix exists, and are already shopping.

### The Siphon Model

Most outbound tries to do two jobs in one message: convince a stranger they have a problem, and convince them you're the fix. Two conversions in one cold email. The maths never works.

The Siphon Model deletes the first job.

Competitor audience → your offer → client.

You're not creating demand. You're redirecting demand that already exists. These people are pre-qualified by behavior, not by a filter you set in a database.

| Signal | Where to find it | Why they're warm |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Engages with competitor content | LinkedIn followers, commenters, post reactors | Actively researching the category right now |
| Visits your profile | LinkedIn profile viewers | Self-identified interest, zero cost to you |
| Attends category events | Conference attendee posts, webinar registrants | Spent time and money on the problem |
| Hiring for the role you replace | LinkedIn job posts, job boards | They've budgeted for the problem |
| Uses an adjacent tool | Job posts naming the tool, review sites | Already bought into the category |
| Comments on the problem publicly | Post comments, category discussions | Told you the problem out loud |

### Siphon Audiences Prompt

You are helping me identify in-market audiences for a B2B service business.

**MY BUSINESS**
- What I sell: [describe your offer in one sentence]
- Who I sell to: [ICP: industry, company size, revenue, role of buyer]
- The problem I solve, in my client's words: [paste actual language clients use]
- Price point: [$X]

**TASK**

Identify 5 "siphon audiences" for me. A siphon audience is a group of people who have already demonstrated, through behavior, that they are in-market for a solution in my category.

For each one, give me:
1. The audience (be specific: not "founders" but "founders who engaged with [category] content in the last 30 days")
2. The behavioral signal that proves they're in-market
3. Exactly where I find them on LinkedIn (be tactical: post commenters, event attendee lists, job posters, profile viewers, group members)
4. What they currently believe about the problem
5. The one thing that would make them stop and read a message from a stranger

**RULES**
- No generic segments. If I could have written it myself in 5 seconds, it's not specific enough.
- Rank them by how close to a buying decision they are.
- Do not suggest audiences I'd have to educate from zero.

Run that once. Pick the top three. That's your target list.

Never name a competitor. Never attack one.

Blue doesn't talk about red. It just shows up blue.

The moment you mention a competitor you've made the conversation about them and you've cast yourself as the challenger. Show up with better proof, lower friction, and a faster time to result. Let that do the arguing.

By the end of this section you'll be able to research a prospect and write a message worth reading in under three minutes, instead of the forty-five it takes manually.

### Prospect Research

This is where Claude earns its place. Not writing your content. Doing the research nobody has time for.

Personalization fails for one reason: people personalize the wrong thing. Mentioning that someone went to Leeds University is not personalization. It's proof you looked at their profile for four seconds. It insults them.

Real personalization means demonstrating that you understand their situation before you ask for anything.

### Prospect Research Prompt

You are researching a prospect before I reach out. Be ruthless about relevance.

**PROSPECT**
[Paste their LinkedIn profile, company About page, and any recent posts]

**MY OFFER**
[One sentence: what I do and the outcome I produce]

**TASK**

Give me:
1. Their likely current bottleneck, based on evidence in what I pasted. Cite the evidence.
2. What they are probably trying to achieve in the next 6-12 months.
3. Three things they've said or done that suggest they'd be receptive to my offer right now.
4. One thing that would make this a bad fit. Be honest. I'd rather not waste the call.
5. The single most relevant angle for a first message, and why it beats the other options.

**RULES**
- Only use evidence from what I pasted. Do not speculate beyond it and do not invent facts about this person.
- If there isn't enough signal to justify outreach, say so plainly.

That last rule matters more than the rest. A research pass that tells you not to reach out has just saved you an hour.

### Cold Message Prompt

Write a cold LinkedIn message to this prospect.

**CONTEXT**
[Paste the output from Prompt 1]

**MY PROOF**
[Paste one real client result: starting point, outcome, timeframe. If you don't have one, say so and I'll use a different structure.]

**RULES**
- Lead with the case study, not with an introduction. Nobody cares who I am yet.
- Maximum 5 sentences.
- No "hope this finds you well." No "I came across your profile." No compliments.
- The personalization must be about their situation, not their biography.
- End with a low-friction ask: 10 minutes, not a demo, not a discovery call.
- Do not invent any numbers, clients, or outcomes. Use only what I gave you.

Give me 3 versions with genuinely different angles, and tell me which you'd send and why.

### Follow-Up Prompt

This prospect didn't reply to my message.

**WHAT I SENT**
[Paste it]

**WHAT I KNOW ABOUT THEM**
[Paste research from Prompt 1]

**TASK**

Write follow-up 2 and follow-up 3.

**RULES**
- Each follow-up must add new value. Never "just bumping this."
- Follow-up 2: a different angle on the same problem, or a piece of proof they haven't seen.
- Follow-up 3: permission-based close. Give them a graceful exit. It should be easy to say "not now" and that should leave the door open.
- No guilt. No fake urgency. No "did you see my last message?"
- 4 sentences max each.

Three prompts. That's the entire outreach engine.

### The Low-Friction Offer

Run the maths before you read another line.

Ten qualified calls this month on a $20K offer.

At a 20% close rate: 2 clients. $40K.

At a 40% close rate: 4 clients. $80K.

Same leads. Same calls. Same effort. Double the revenue.

Most people respond to this by trying to double the lead volume instead. That costs money, time, and a hire. Fixing the offer costs a week of thinking.

A well-built low-friction offer puts close rates in the 30-40% range. It's the highest-leverage work available to you and almost nobody does it.

| Friction source | What it sounds like | The fix |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Too much commitment | 12-month retainer, large upfront | A small first step that's a real win on its own |
| Needs explaining | "Let me walk you through our framework" | Lead with the outcome, not the mechanism |
| Risk sits with them | "Results in 3 to 6 months" | Compress time to first result, or carry the risk |
| It's about you | "We're a leading provider of..." | Speak to what the market already wants |
| Too many decisions | Three tiers, add-ons, options | One offer. One price. One yes. |

Pull your last 20 sales calls.

Write down the exact words prospects used for their problem. Theirs, not yours. "We need more deals." "We can't scale." "We're too dependent on referrals."

Pull your best client results, the ones you'd stake your reputation on.

Find the overlap. That overlap is your offer.

### Offer Stress-Test Prompt

You are pressure-testing a B2B offer. Your job is to find the friction, not to be encouraging.

**MY OFFER**
- What it is: [describe it]
- Price: [$X]
- Who it's for: [ICP]
- What they get: [deliverables]
- Time to first result: [X days/weeks]
- Guarantee or risk reversal, if any: [describe]

**TASK**

1. Score the friction 1-10 (10 = a stranger could say yes in 60 seconds).
2. List every reason a qualified prospect would hesitate. Rank by how often it would come up.
3. For each hesitation, give me a specific structural fix. Not better copy. A change to the offer itself.
4. Rewrite my offer as a single sentence a stranger could say yes to in 60 seconds without consulting anyone.
5. Tell me what I'd be giving up with that rewrite, and whether it's worth it.

**RULES**
- Be adversarial. Assume the prospect is skeptical, busy, and has been burned by someone in my category before.
- Do not compliment the offer. Find what's broken.

The low-friction offer isn't the business. It's the raised hand.

A small entry commitment tells you someone is serious. That's information you cannot get any other way, and it's worth more than a hundred "interested" replies. From there a client who's had a real win escalates naturally. The small buyer becomes the $8K buyer becomes the $20K buyer becomes the $50K engagement. Each step earned by a result, not sold by a pitch.

Build one offer. Build it for scale from day one. A single well-constructed offer can carry a business to $100K/month, $500K/month, and beyond.

### The 10-Minute Triage Call

The triage call is not a sales call. It's a diagnostic. You're there to find out whether you can help, and to collect the exact language you'll use later to prove it.

Ten minutes. Five questions. No deck.

1. Where do you want the business to be in 6 to 12 months?
2. What are you currently doing to get there?
3. What's stopping you?
4. How long has this been going on?
5. Are you profitable?

That's the entire script.

| Question | What you're really finding out |
| --- | --- |
| Where do you want to be | The gap. Everything you sell later gets framed against this number. |
| What are you doing | Their current mechanism, and whether it's broken or just under-resourced. |
| What's stopping you | The bottleneck, in their words. Write it down verbatim. |
| How long has it been going on | Urgency. Six months is a project. Two years is a wound. |
| Are you profitable | Whether they can actually buy. Ask it plainly. |

Write down their exact language. Word for word. Not a paraphrase.

When you hand them a document later that uses their own sentences back to them, they don't feel sold to. They feel understood. That's the whole trick, and it's the gap between a 20% close rate and a 40% one.

Don't pitch. Promise a document.

> "Based on what you've told me, I'll put together a strategy document mapping exactly how you get from where you are to [their stated goal]. I'll walk you through it. When works for you?"

Then book the next call on the call. Not "I'll send some times."

Routing rule: anything under $10K can close in the DMs. Anything above $10K needs a call. Match the channel to the price point and stop trying to close five-figure deals over LinkedIn messages.

### Strategy Map Selling

Stop selling services. Sell strategy.

Nobody wakes up wanting to buy an acquisition system. They want to know how they get from £80K a month to £300K a month without hiring twelve people and losing their weekends. Sell them the map. The services are just how the map gets executed.

After triage, you build a document. Not a proposal. A map.

| Section | What goes in it |
| --- | --- |
| The mechanism | How you solve this, in one page, plus why you're credible |
| Ramp-up time | Exactly how long until the first result. Be specific. |
| The deliverables | Step by step, each tied explicitly to a pain they named on triage |
| Predicted results | What happens if they execute, with the maths shown |
| Proof | Results from businesses that look like theirs |
| Investment | The number. Plainly. No games. |
| Urgency | Natural, not manufactured. Real constraints only. |

The deliverables section is where the triage call pays off. Every deliverable maps to something they said. If they said "we're too dependent on referrals," there's a line in the map that says exactly how that dependency ends, in their words.

### Strategy Map Prompt

You are drafting a strategy document for a prospect after a triage call. This is not a proposal. It's a map from where they are to where they said they want to be.

**TRIAGE NOTES (their exact words)**
- 6-12 month goal: [verbatim]
- What they're currently doing: [verbatim]
- What's stopping them: [verbatim]
- How long it's been going on: [verbatim]
- Profitable: [yes/no, plus context]

**MY SOLUTION**
- Mechanism: [how you solve this]
- Deliverables: [list them]
- Time to first result: [X]
- Investment: [$X]
- Relevant proof: [real client results only]

**TASK**

Draft the strategy map with these sections in order:
1. The mechanism and why I'm credible on it
2. Ramp-up time
3. Deliverables, each one explicitly tied to a pain they named
4. Predicted results, with the maths shown
5. Proof
6. Investment
7. Natural urgency

**RULES**
- Use THEIR language throughout. If they said "we can't scale," the document says "you can't scale," not "scalability constraints."
- Every deliverable must connect to something they actually said. If a deliverable doesn't map to a stated pain, cut it.
- Do not invent proof, numbers, or client names. Use only what I gave you.
- No hype adjectives. The maths should do the persuading.

Here's what makes this work.

You send the map before the call, with a short video walking through it and a timeline.

By the time they show up they've read it, watched the video, and shown it to their business partner. They've already imagined it running in their business.

They arrive sold on the solution. The call isn't a pitch. It's an onboarding conversation that ends with a signature.

Even when a call comes in referral-quality and pre-sold, sit down in that seat as though the prospect has never heard of you. Assume nothing. Earn it from zero, every time.

That discipline is what produced this system, and it's what keeps the close rate where it is.
