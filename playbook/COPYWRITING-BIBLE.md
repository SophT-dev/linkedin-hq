# PART A — SUGARMAN: *The Adweek Copywriting Handbook*

The most useful copy manual ever written. Sugarman built long-copy mail-order ads that sold planes, thermostats, and 20M pairs of BluBlocker sunglasses. His whole system is one idea: **get them to read the next sentence — over and over — until they act.** That is exactly the LinkedIn dwell-time game.

## A§1 · The 15 Axioms (memorize these)

These are his laws. Every one maps onto LinkedIn.

1. **Copywriting is a mental process** — the sum of all your experience + specific knowledge, transferred to the page to sell. *(You can't fake it; feed the machine.)*
2. **All the elements of an ad exist to do ONE thing: get you to read the first sentence.** *(On LinkedIn: the profile pic, the name, the first ~2 visible lines, the visual — all exist to earn the "…see more" click.)*
3. **The sole purpose of the first sentence is to get you to read the second sentence.** *(And the second sells the third. This is the whole game.)*
4. **Your layout + first paragraphs must create the buying environment** most conducive to the sale. *(Tone, formatting, white space = the "room" you walk the reader into.)*
5. **Get the reader to say "yes" and harmonize** with your truthful statements as they read. *(Open with something they already believe/agree with — get the internal nod.)*
6. **The Slippery Slide** — readers should be so compelled they can't stop until they've read it all.
7. **Don't assume constraints that aren't really there.** *(The "elephant on a chain." Most limits on what a post can do are imagined. "Nobody reads long posts" is an assumed constraint.)*
8. **Keep the reader interested through curiosity.**
9. **Never sell a product/service. Always sell a concept.** *(Sell the sizzle, the Big Idea, the positioning — not the feature list.)*
10. **The incubation process** — your subconscious solves the problem while you step away. Load the brain, then walk.
11. **Copy should be long enough to cause the reader to take the action you request** — no longer, no shorter. *(There's no "right length." There's "did they act.")*
12. **Every communication should be personal** — writer → one recipient — regardless of medium. *(Write to ONE person. "You," "I," "me." Never "you all.")*
13. **Copy should flow logically, anticipating the prospect's questions and answering them** as if asked face-to-face.
14. **In editing, refine the copy to express exactly what you want in the fewest words.**
15. **Sell a cure, not a preventive** — unless you can make the preventive feel like a cure.

## A§2 · The pre-writing engine (do this before you type)

- **General knowledge** — the copywriter's edge is a broad, curious life. Read widely, collect offbeat stories. *(This is why we run INSIDER-RESEARCH.md — each line is fuel.)*
- **Specific knowledge** — become an expert on the exact thing you're selling before you write a word. Sugarman spent *days* in a watch lab to find "Laser Beam Digital Watch." **For us: know the tool, the tactic, the number cold before we post about it.**
- **Know your customer** — you must be an expert on who you're talking to. *(Our ICP: founders/agencies doing cold outbound.)*
- **Understand the product's nature** — every product has an inherent nature and a *right* way to be presented (serious vs. fun vs. prestige). Match the treatment to the nature.
- **Practice, practice, practice** — volume is the teacher. First drafts are supposed to be terrible.
- **The Incubation Process** — load all the inputs, then STOP and do something pleasurable. The subconscious assembles the Big Idea. Pressure (deadline, ego) speeds it up. Right brain writes; hold back the left-brain (grammar) until editing.

## A§3 · The Slippery Slide — the core mechanic (this IS LinkedIn)

**Every element must be so compelling you slide down and can't stop until the end.** Build it like this:

1. **First sentence: make it SHORT.** So short it's "almost not a sentence." His real openers:
   - *"Losing weight is not easy."*
   - *"It's you against a computer."*
   - *"It's easy."*
   - *"It had to happen."*
   - *"Hats off to IBM."*
   > **Steal for LinkedIn hooks.** The first line is the whole ballgame — it's the visible line before "…see more." Make it a 3–6 word gut-punch. (Ties to Michel's "first line answers *why should I care*" in [PLAYBOOK.md](PLAYBOOK.md).)
2. **Each sentence sells the next.** No benefits yet — the ONLY job of the opening is momentum.
3. **Seeds of Curiosity** — end paragraphs with tiny forward-pulls so they can't stop:
   - *"But there's more."*  ·  *"So read on."*  ·  *"But I didn't stop there."*  ·  *"Let me explain."*  ·  *"Now here comes the good part."*  ·  *"And here's why."*
   > **Steal these verbatim** as line-breaks in LinkedIn posts. They're the print version of the TV "when we come back…" cliffhanger. Don't overdo it.
4. **The 25% rule** — if a reader gets past ~25% of your copy, they'll very likely finish. So the opening is where 90% of the battle is.
5. **Create the buying environment** in the first few lines — the tone/room that makes the reader comfortable to act. Discount = busy + big numbers; premium = clean + calm + confident.
6. **Harmony / the "yes" ladder** — open with statements the reader can only nod at (true, relatable, in their language). One "no" and they're gone. Get the internal *yes, yes, yes* before you ask for anything.
7. **Start with a story or an offbeat news item.** Sugarman opened ads with an embezzler, British underwear stats — anything to grease the slide, then bridged to the product. **Stories are the #1 way in.**

## A§4 · Sell the CONCEPT, not the product (Axiom 9)

- "You sell the sizzle, not the steak." Find the **Big Idea / USP / positioning** — the single angle that makes it stand out.
- Examples: a smoke detector sold as **"The Nose"** (a nose on your ceiling that sniffs for smoke). A walkie-talkie on a CB frequency became **"Pocket CB."** A phone-directory calculator became **"Pocket Yellow Pages."** A chess computer became **"Soviet Challenge"** (challenge the Russian champion — not "buy a chess computer").
- **Price itself is a concept:** same Pocket CB felt like a serious radio at $39.95, a gadget at $29.95, a toy at $19.95 — same copy.
- **Exception:** if the product is genuinely new, the product *is* the concept (just explain it). Once it's commoditized, you need a fresh concept per ad.
> **For us:** never post "here's a tool." Post the concept: *"the 3-tool stack that replaces a $4k/mo SDR."* Same info, wrapped in an idea. This is literally our F2 format.

## A§5 · The 23 Copy Elements — use as a pre-publish checklist

Sugarman's checklist for what a complete sales message covers. Run a draft against it.

| # | Element | The rule | LinkedIn application |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Typeface** | Serif = 5× comprehension over sans in his tests. Legibility first. | We can't pick LI's font, but: short lines, whitespace, no walls of text. |
| 2 | **First sentence** | Short, easy, compelling → forces sentence 2. | The hook line. |
| 3 | **Second sentence** | Almost as important; keeps momentum. | Line 2 must earn the "…see more." |
| 4 | **Paragraph headings** | Break copy into chunks so it looks un-intimidating (can even be nonsense — they still work). | Line breaks + one-line "paragraphs." Scannability. |
| 5 | **Product explanation** | Explain a *complicated* product **simply**; a *simple* product in a **complex/interesting** way. | Make a boring tactic sound deep; make a scary AI thing sound simple. |
| 6 | **New features** | Flag what's genuinely new/unique. | "First," "new," the differentiator. |
| 7 | **Technical explanation** | Detail builds trust — "we like to buy from an expert." Use terms that prove you did the work. | Drop the specific mechanism/number that shows we actually run this. |
| 8 | **Anticipate objections** | Sense the next doubt and raise it yourself. | "You're thinking this only works for big teams. It doesn't — here's why." |
| 9 | **Resolve objections** | Raise, then dispel honestly. | Every post that makes a claim should pre-empt the "yeah but." |
| 10 | **Gender / audience** | Know exactly who you talk to; don't offend. | Speak our ICP's language, not generic "entrepreneur." |
| 11 | **Clarity** | Clear, simple, short, no big words (unless it's the technical-expert move). | Fog Index low (A§8). |
| 12 | **Clichés** | Ban them. "The product the world's been waiting for" = you have nothing to say. | No "game-changer," no "in today's fast-paced world." |
| 13 | **Rhythm** | Vary sentence length. Short. Short. Then a long one. Use the **triad** (list of 3). | Reads out loud like breathing. Triads everywhere. |
| 14 | **Service** | Address "what if it breaks / what's the catch." | For lead magnets: "free, no call required, no catch." |
| 15 | **Physical facts** | Give EVERY spec. Any missing fact = an excuse not to act. | Give the real numbers, dates, names. Gaps kill. |
| 16 | **Trial period** | Longer = fewer returns + more trust. | "Steal the whole doc, use it, keep it either way." |
| 17 | **Price comparison** | Anchor value against a competitor/alternative. | "An SDR is $4k/mo. This is a $0 template." |
| 18 | **Testimonials** | Real names + towns = credible. Celebs get recalled but overshadow the product. **Expert** testimonials beat celeb. | Client screenshots, real names, real results (blur sensitive per our rule). |
| 19 | **Price** | Put it where the eye lands; big type if it's the selling point, underplay if not. | The "ask" (opt-in/CTA) placed clearly. |
| 20 | **Offer summary** | Restate the whole offer near the end. | "So: comment 'X' and I'll send the full sequence." |
| 21 | **Avoid saying too much** | Biggest student mistake. Cut 50–80%. | Ruthless editing. |
| 22 | **Ease of ordering** | Make responding dead simple. Dotted-line coupon pulled best. | One clear CTA, one action, minimal friction. |
| 23 | **Ask for the order** | ALWAYS explicitly ask. Most forget. | Every post ends with ONE clear CTA. Never trail off. |

## A§6 · The 31 Psychological Triggers — the idea generator

**This is the single most valuable list in the book.** Run any topic against all 31 to find angles. Each below: the trigger + how we weaponize it on LinkedIn.

1. **Involvement / Ownership** — make them mentally hold/use it. *("Picture your inbox Monday morning: 12 booked calls, zero manual sending.")* Involvement devices (the spelling-error ad) 2–3× response.
2. **Honesty** — the single most important. Consumers are smarter collectively than you. State the flaws up front; it disarms and sells. *(Our "here's what didn't work" posts.)*
3. **Integrity** — conveyed by the *look* and tone. Clean, honest layout > shouting.
4. **Credibility** — believability. Resolve every objection; borrow credibility from where you post + who you cite (the "piggyback" — QVC/WSJ effect). *(Post proof; tag real brands.)*
5. **Value & Proof of Value** — educating the reader = lowering the price. Show the math.
6. **Justify the Purchase** — give the logical excuse for the emotional decision. *("You deserve to stop doing this manually.")*
7. **Greed** — bargains move people even when they don't need the thing. *(Over-deliver on the free lead magnet.)*
8. **Establish Authority** — say something that positions you as THE expert/category leader ("America's largest single source of…"). *(Taha = the cold-outbound authority.)*
9. **Satisfaction Conviction** — go *beyond* a guarantee, so they think "how can they afford to do that?" ("Keep it either way, plus interest.") **Doubled his response when tested.**
10. **Nature of Product** — serious product = serious tone; fun = fun.
11. **Prospect Nature** — sell to the buyer's real emotional need (the fraternity story: guys join to meet people + belong).
12. **Current Fads** — ride what's hot *now* (but don't over-attach; fads die). *(Ride the AI/Claude wave while it's peaking.)*
13. **Timing** — right idea, wrong moment = death. Test; let the market tell you.
14. **Linking** — bridge the new/unknown to something familiar. Smoke detector → "nose." *(New AI workflow → "a junior SDR that never sleeps.")* **Huge for explaining anything new.**
15. **Consistency** — get a tiny commitment first; people act consistently with it. *(A "comment 'X'" micro-commitment primes the opt-in.)*
16. **Harmonize** — meet the market where it already is (Bobby Darin wrote "Splish Splash" to fit the market, *then* did his own thing).
17. **Desire to Belong** — people buy to join a group/identity (Mercedes, Marlboro). *("the operators who've stopped renting SDRs")*.
18. **Desire to Collect** — people collect; your best buyer is a past buyer. *(Series/multi-part content, "part 2 of…")*.
19. **Curiosity** — THE strongest motivator in mail order (and on a feed). Hold back the payoff. BluBlocker never showed the view through the lens — you had to buy. *(Tease the mechanism; deliver in the lead magnet.)*
20. **Sense of Urgency** — give a real reason to act now (out of sight = out of mind). Never fake it — kills integrity.
21. **Fear** — loss/missing out drives action (crime, aging, FOMO). Use carefully.
22. **Instant Gratification** — retail's edge; neutralize it by delivering fast. *(Lead magnet arrives instantly.)*
23. **Exclusivity / Rarity / Uniqueness** — "only 6 in the state" made Sugarman buy a snowmobile he didn't need. *(Cohort caps, "closing the list.")*
24. **Simplicity** — one clear offer beats nine choices (his Swiss-Army-watch A/B: 1 watch beat 9 watches **3:1**). Keep copy, layout, and the ask simple.
25. **Human Relationships** — resonate like a tuning fork; add the human element (a hand holding the product, a face, a story).
26. **Storytelling** — people are primed for stories since childhood. His biggest campaigns were all stories. **Open with one.**
27. **Mental Engagement** — make the mind do a little work to reach the conclusion *itself* — that's more persuasive than spelling it out. (The Seiko "jewelers make a fortune" line he *didn't* write.) Hemingway never over-described. *(Imply the punchline; let them land it.)*
28. **Guilt** — over-give value and they feel they owe you a response. *(Free, genuinely useful posts → reciprocity.)*
29. **Specificity** — "92% of dentists" beats "dentists everywhere." Exact facts/figures = credibility + expert signal. **"237 miles of blood vessels," not "miles."** *(Our whole edge: $942K pipeline, 55% reply rate — never "great results.")*
30. **Familiarity** — repetition + recognizable format/brand = trust. Don't kill a winning campaign because *you're* bored (the public isn't). Familiar words: people pick **7** and **red** most often. *(Consistent brand look; recurring formats.)*
31. **Hope** — implied future benefit (creams, vitamins). Never make a *measurable* promise you can't guarantee (the "$20k formula" scientist's fatal error). Imply via testimonial; anchor on credibility.

## A§7 · Editing (Axiom 14) — where good becomes great

- **Goal: express exactly what you mean in the fewest words.** First draft = emotional dump (right brain). Editing = nurture it (left brain).
- His 5 editing moves:
  1. **Kill "that" words** — "you know *that*…" → cut the whole run-up.
  2. **Edit for rhythm** — vary sentence length so it isn't monotone.
  3. **Combine sentences** where it tightens.
  4. **Eliminate unnecessary words** — every "the," "very," filler.
  5. **Rearrange for flow** — lead with the emotional pleasure, then explain.
- Cut extraneous preambles ("Finally, it is important to note that…" → delete).
- **Time is an edit tool** — sleep on it; the subconscious flags the weak parts.
- **John Caples changed one word — "repair" → "fix" — and lifted response 20%.** Word choice is not cosmetic.

**The 7 Steps to Writing Great Copy (his summary formula):**
1. Become an expert on the product. 2. Know your prospect. 3. Write headline + subhead (short; sub ~16 words). 4. Write the copy — dump everything, don't edit yet. 5. Edit — tighten, cut. 6. Incubate — walk away. 7. Final pass.

## A§8 · Clarity: the Fog Index

Lower grade level = wider audience. Best-sellers = 8th–10th grade; WSJ = 11th. His best ad (BluBlocker "Vision Breakthrough") opened at a **~5th-grade** level. Method: avg sentence length + % of 3-syllable words, ×0.4. **Takeaway for us: short sentences, small words. Simple = broad reach = more response.** Don't obsess over the score; use short words and vary length.

## A§9 · Cure vs. Prevention (Axiom 15)

- **A cure sells far easier — and for far more — than a preventive.** People think they'll never get the disease. Once they have it, they'll pay anything.
- **Turn a preventive into a cure:** emphasize the curative angle. His fuel-additive pill: don't sell "prevents engine problems" (prevention) — sell "cures engine knock, kills ping, passes your emissions test" (cure).
> **For us:** don't post "prevent low reply rates" (prevention, weak). Post "fix your dead cold-email campaign" / "why your replies dried up and how to bring them back" (cure, strong). Target the people who already feel the pain.

# PART B — OGILVY: *Ogilvy on Advertising*

Ogilvy is the evidence. Where Sugarman gives you the *engine*, Ogilvy gives you the *data* on what actually pulls, tested across decades and countries. His posture: advertising is **information, not art**; the job is to sell.

## B§1 · Headlines (the money muscle)

**"On average, 5× as many people read the headline as the body. If your headline doesn't sell, you've wasted 90% of your money."** On LinkedIn, the headline = the first line. Rules:

- **Promise a benefit.** Headlines that promise a benefit are read by **4× more** people (Starch). *"Promise, large promise, is the soul of an advertisement." —Samuel Johnson.* Ogilvy calls this the single most important sentence in his book.
- **News pulls.** "New," "introducing," "now," "suddenly," "announcing." Ads with news are recalled by **22% more** people. A new product OR a new way to use an old one.
- **Be specific, not vague.** "Sears makes a profit of 5%" beat "less than you might suppose." Specifics are more credible and memorable.
- **Put the headline in quotes → +28% recall.**
- **Flag your audience** if niche — put the word that stops the right person ("asthma," "founders," "cold email," "agencies over $1M").
- **Include the brand/subject** — 80% only read the headline; if it's not there, they never learn what it's about.
- **Long or short both work** — write whatever length the idea needs. (One-word "Lemon" worked; so did 10-word retail headlines.)
- **Avoid tricky/blind headlines** — puns, double meanings, headlines that don't say what it's about = ~20% below average recall. In a feed your line competes with hundreds; **telegraph** the message.
- **"How to…" pulls above average.** ("How to win friends…")
- **Never run a headless ad.** No hook = no post.

## B§2 · The Big Idea + Positioning + Brand Image

- **"Unless your advertising contains a big idea, it will pass like a ship in the night."** Ogilvy had ~20 in his whole career. The 5 questions to test one:
  1. Did it make me gasp? 2. Do I wish I'd thought of it? 3. Is it unique? 4. Does it fit the strategy perfectly? 5. **Could it run for 30 years?**
- **Positioning = "what the product does, and who it's for."** Dove = a toilet bar for women with dry skin (not a soap for dirty men). SAAB = "the car for winter." Same product, different position = different result.
- **Brand image = personality**, built consistently, year after year. The image *is* 90% of what you sell (whiskey drinkers taste the *image*). *(For us: Taha's + Sophiya's consistent voice/look IS the moat.)*
- **"The positively good."** You don't have to prove you're *better* than competitors — just clearly, honestly, informatively **good**. Confidence wins the swing.
- **Make the product the hero.** "There are no dull products, only dull writers."
- **Do your homework.** Ogilvy read 3 weeks on Rolls-Royce → found "At 60 mph the loudest noise is the electric clock." Homework = the raw material of big ideas.
- **Repeat your winners.** Readership doesn't decline over ≥4 repetitions. "You're advertising to a moving parade, not a standing army." *(Don't retire a format because we're bored — see Trigger 30.)*
- **Down with committees.** "Search the parks in all your cities / You'll find no statues of committees." Great work isn't designed by consensus.

## B§3 · Body Copy

- **Write to ONE person.** "When people read your copy, they are alone… one human being to another, second person singular." (Same as Sugarman Axiom 12.) Be Disraeli, not Gladstone-addressing-a-meeting.
- **"You cannot bore people into buying. You can only interest them."**
- **Short sentences, short paragraphs, simple words.** He changed "obsolete" → "old-fashioned" because housewives didn't know it.
- **Tell it as a STORY.** "The amazing story of a Zippo that worked after being taken from the belly of a fish." Caples' "They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano…"
- **First paragraph must be a grabber.** Never open with the obvious ("Going on vacation is a pleasure everyone looks forward to"). Compare the Harvard prof's opener: *"Cesare Borgia murdered his brother-in-law for the love of his sister, who was the mistress of their father — the Pope."*
- **Specifics sell.** "The more facts you tell, the more you sell." Avoid "brag and boast" superlatives ("the best in the world") — they convince nobody.
- **Avoid analogies** — widely misunderstood.
- **Testimonials** make copy more credible (real consumers/experts > anonymous puffery > celebrities, who get remembered while the product is forgotten).
- **Markdowns/special offers** are above average in recall (consumers like them even when copywriters think they're boring).
- **Always include the price** on real-money items — no price = they turn the page.
- **Long copy sells** for considered purchases — "the more facts, the more you sell." Long copy also *signals* you have something important to say. But **write it well** or nobody reads it.

## B§4 · Illustrations / Visuals (for our 1080×1350s + GIFs)

Ogilvy's tested rules — apply to every visual we design:

- **Story appeal wins.** A photo that makes you ask *"what's going on here?"* pulls readers into the copy. (The Hathaway eyepatch. This is why our visuals shouldn't just be a logo grid.)
- **Show the end-result / before-and-after.** In 70 campaigns Gallup never found a before-after that *didn't* increase sales.
- **The subject/idea of the visual matters more than the craft** — a great photographer can't save a boring idea.
- **Captions are read by ~4× more people than body copy** — never run an image without a caption, and put the brand + promise in it. *(For us: the text baked onto the visual = the caption. Make it carry the promise.)*
- **Keep it simple** — one focus of interest; crowd scenes don't pull.
- **Photos > drawings** for believability/recall (except where reproduction is bad).
- **Don't assume what interests you interests them** (Ogilvy's chef photos flopped with housewives). Test.
- **Editorial > "ad-looking."** (See B§5 — this is the biggest one for LinkedIn.)

**Format reach multipliers** (cross-checked against an external LinkedIn content-system guide,
2026-07-08) — pick the format deliberately, it changes reach by multiples:

| Format | Reach multiplier |
|---|---|
| Carousel (PDF) | 2.5-3.5x |
| Native video | 1.2-2x |
| Text-only | 1x (baseline) |
| External link in the post body | 0.3-0.6x — kills reach |

**Rule, no exceptions: never put a URL in the post body.** The link always goes in the first
comment, posted immediately after the post goes live. This is why the lead-magnet CTA is phrased
as "comment [keyword] and I'll send it" rather than "link below" — the mechanic already routes
around this correctly, but it's worth stating explicitly as a hard rule for every future post.

## B§5 · Layout & Typography — "make it look editorial, not like an ad"

**Ogilvy's most transferable insight for a social feed:** *"There is no law that advertisements have to look like advertisements. If you make them look like editorial pages, you'll attract more readers."* ~6× as many people read the average article as the average ad.

- **Scan order = illustration → headline → copy.** Put them in that order (headline UNDER the illustration → read by 10% more people).
- **Serif type, black-on-white.** Never reverse (white on black) for long copy — he found 47 unreadable reverse ads in one issue. Never all-caps headlines (caps retard reading — no ascenders/descenders). No period at the end of a headline (full stops = "stop reading").
- **Drop-initial to open → +13% readership. Leading between paragraphs → +12%. Cross-heads** keep the reader marching (make some interrogative, to spark curiosity into the next chunk).
- **Opening paragraph ≤ 11 words.**
- **Number unrelated facts** (like this list) instead of clunky connectives. Use bullets, arrowheads, bold/italic for key lines. Widows (short lines) *increase* readership.
- **KISS — Keep It Simple, Stupid.** The drama belongs in *what you say*, not the typeface.
- **"God is in the details."**
> **For us:** our LinkedIn posts already win by looking like a person talking, not a brand broadcasting. The formatting rules above = why. Lead-magnet landing pages should read like an article/editorial, not a sales page.

## B§6 · Long vs. Short + TV/emotion crossovers

- **Direct-response is the truth-teller.** DR advertisers *know* what sold. They use: **long copy, 2-minute (not 30-sec) spots, late-night airtime.** "Every copywriter should spend two years in direct response." Long copy invariably out-sells short in split-run tests. **The more pertinent facts, the more you sell.** *(Validates our long, value-dense posts + long lead magnets.)*
- From his TV chapter (transferable to video/GIF/reels): **open with the fire** (grab in the first frame — the feed equivalent of the first line), **show the product in use / the end-result**, **demonstrations persuade**, **problem→solution works**, **brand ID early** (name it in the first seconds), **humor now sells** (once forbidden), **emotion sells** (nostalgia/charm) — but **always give a rational excuse to justify the emotional decision** (= Sugarman's "sell on emotion, justify with logic"). **Avoid visual banality** — show something they've never seen.
- **Celebrity endorsers underperform** (people remember the celebrity, forget the product) — **expert** endorsers persuade.

## B§7 · Lessons from the greats (Ogilvy's heroes)

- **Claude Hopkins** (*Scientific Advertising* — "read it 7 times"): *"Ad writers forget they are salesmen and try to be performers. Instead of sales, they seek applause."* · Test everything, don't argue around a table. · A headline change can multiply returns 5–10×. · Give each advertiser a "becoming style" (brand image, before the term existed). · **"People don't buy from clowns"** (later disproved — humor can sell now, but the caution stands: entertain in service of selling).
- **Bill Bernbach:** worshipped originality + making the product the hero. *"Human nature hasn't changed for a billion years… The creative man with an insight into human nature, with the artistry to touch and move people, will succeed."* Card in his pocket: **"Maybe he's right."**
- **John Caples** (direct-response doyen): one ad out-pulled another **19½×** — same space, same everything, different **appeal**. The appeal (the promise/angle) is everything.
- **Rosser Reeves:** *"Do you want fine writing? Do you want masterpieces? Or do you want to see the goddamned sales curve start moving up?"* Originality for its own sake = *ignis fatuus* (swamp fire).
- **Leo Burnett:** *"When you reach for the stars, you may not quite get one, but you won't come up with a handful of mud either."*
- **Benton & Bowles:** *"If it doesn't sell, it isn't creative."*

# PART D — SYNTHESIS: where both masters agree (our operating rules)

When Ogilvy (data) and Sugarman (craft) **independently land on the same rule**, treat it as law:

1. **The opening is 90% of the job.** Headline (Ogilvy) = first sentence (Sugarman) = the "…see more" line (us). Make it short, benefit/curiosity-loaded, specific.
2. **Write to ONE person.** Second person singular. Personal > corporate. (Ogilvy's Disraeli = Sugarman's Axiom 12.)
3. **Specificity = credibility.** Real numbers, names, facts. Kill vague superlatives and clichés. (Ogilvy's "Sears 5%" = Sugarman's "92% of dentists.")
4. **Sell emotion, justify with logic.** Both say it outright. Give the feeling *and* the rational excuse.
5. **Story is the best way in.** Both built their biggest wins on stories.
6. **Long copy works IF every line earns the next.** Facts sell (Ogilvy); the slippery slide keeps them reading (Sugarman). Length isn't the enemy — boredom is.
7. **Homework/expertise is non-negotiable.** You can't write it if you don't know it cold.
8. **Do the ONE thing: ask for the action, make it easy.** Every piece ends with a single clear CTA.
9. **Consistency + repetition build the brand.** Don't kill a winner because you're bored; the parade is always new.
10. **Test, don't argue.** Let the audience (engagement, replies, opt-ins) decide — not opinion.

## Our LinkedIn pre-publish checklist (run every post/hook against this)

- [ ] **First line** — short, specific, benefit or curiosity? Would it stop *our ICP* mid-scroll? Does it force line 2?
- [ ] **Hook fits before "...see more" truncation** — LinkedIn cuts the post preview at roughly 140 characters on mobile (range 140-210 depending on screen size) and roughly 200-250 on desktop (~210 most commonly cited). Neither number is LinkedIn's own official spec — both are third-party observed and could shift — so treat **120-130 visible characters as the hard ceiling for the hook**, conservative enough to survive the tightest mobile fold. Count every space, line break, and emoji: standard emoji cost ~2 characters, flag/skin-tone emoji 4-7. A hook that's fine in the editor can still get chopped mid-word on a small phone if it's packed with emoji. Sources: authoredup.com, linkedhelper.com, taplio.com, postory.io, connectsafely.ai (checked 2026-07-08).
- [ ] **Concept, not product** — is there a Big Idea/angle, or am I just listing a tool/feature?
- [ ] **One person** — "you/I/me," conversational, not "you all"?
- [ ] **Yes-ladder** — do the first lines earn agreement/relatability?
- [ ] **Slippery slide** — does every line pull to the next? Seeds of curiosity between chunks?
- [ ] **Specificity** — real numbers/names/facts, zero vague superlatives or clichés?
- [ ] **Emotion + logic** — a feeling *and* a rational justification?
- [ ] **Story?** — can I open with one?
- [ ] **Objection raised + resolved** — did I pre-empt the "yeah but"?
- [ ] **Trigger check** — which of the 31 am I firing? (Aim for 2–4 deliberately.)
- [ ] **Editing** — cut 30%+? Killed "that"/filler? Varied sentence length? Triads?
- [ ] **Clarity** — short words, low Fog Index, scannable formatting (editorial, not ad-looking)?
- [ ] **Visual** — story appeal + the caption/baked-text carries the promise?
- [ ] **The ask** — ONE clear CTA, easy to act on?
- [ ] **Cure not prevention** — am I talking to people who feel the pain now?
- [ ] **Honesty** — true, no fake urgency, flaws acknowledged?
- [ ] **No link in the body** — is the URL in the first comment, not the post text? (see B§4)

## E§1 · SAM PARR (The Hustle / My First Million / copythat.com)

**Studied from:** copythat.com (his "copywork" method), The Hustle's internal style guide, MFM, and multiple course breakdowns (sources logged in-session 2026-07-02).

**The origin:** he learned by **copywork** — hand-copying great sales letters ~1 hr/day. Philosophy: learn others' songs, then find your own voice.

**Core beliefs**
- **First sentence punches you in the face.** Its only job: make you read sentence 2. (He's a Sugarman disciple — pure slippery slide.)
- **"C+ writing, A+ story/insight."** Never chase pretty prose; chase a wild story or a surprising fact told plainly. Insight > polish.
- **Write like you talk to a friend.** Conversational, informed, clever-not-forced, data-driven-but-accessible, personal-but-universal.

**Sentence mechanics (steal exactly)**
- Sentences ≤ ~25 words; often 3–6. One-word lines for punch ("Nuts."). Paragraphs 1–3 lines, tons of white space. 6th–8th grade level.
- Kill adverbs (-ly) → strong verb instead ("I yelled at the boy" > "angrily yelled").
- Specific numbers ALWAYS. "$27M," "30 days," "6,000 leads" — never "a lot."
- Cut the first & last 25% of the draft, then cut ~50% more.

**Signature vocabulary + connectors (his real idiom)**
- Openers/connectors: **"Here's the deal." · "Ok so…" · "Look…" · "Here's the gist." · "But first." · "I'll explain." · "And boom." · "Long story short."**
- Color/reactions: **"It's nuts." · "This is wild." · "kinda crazy" · "the crazy part?" · "not gonna lie" · "honestly" · "real talk" · "dude / man" · "(seriously)" · "I'm not joking."**
- Moves: rhetorical-question hooks · self-deprecation ("why would I do something this stupid?") · parenthetical asides · a curiosity gap held open till later · money/business-nerd framing ("this tiny thing prints $X").
- **Brand-safe ceiling:** Sam says "I fuck with that guy." We don't — keep the energy, a rare "damn"/"hell" is the max.

**His hook patterns**
- Bold/absurd confession: *"I went 30 days without food. Here's why."*
- Dumb-question hook: *"Why would I do something this stupid?"*
- Surprising number: *"This one-page website makes $40k a month."*
- Setup + withhold: *"A guy paid me to fix his copy. His copy was fine. The problem was way weirder."*
- Contrarian gut-punch: *"Cold email isn't dead. Your list is."*

**Do / Don't**
- **Do:** open with a punch, tell ONE story, real numbers, write like a text to a smart friend, end on one clean line or one clear ask.
- **Don't:** warm up ("In today's world…"), adverbs/jargon/clichés, long sentences, explaining the joke, sounding like a brand.

**Litmus test:** read it out loud. If it sounds like a LinkedIn "thought leader," kill it. If it sounds like Sam telling you something wild over a beer — with a real number in it — you nailed it.

# PART F — HORMOZI + MRBEAST: ATTENTION & CURIOSITY ENGINEERING

**Added 2026-07-08** after Sophiya flagged a hook as flat/uncompelling and asked for the actual
psychology behind grabbing attention, not just craft mechanics. **No Hormozi book or the "Mr.
Beast leaked document" she referenced exist anywhere in this repo** — this section is built from
public research (his published $100M Leads framework, interviews, and reporting on MrBeast's
process), not those specific sources. If she supplies either document, extract it properly and
fold it in here rather than relying on secondhand summaries.

## F§1 · Hormozi's Hook → Retain → Reward framework

Every piece of content does three jobs, in order:
1. **Hook — grab attention.** Pick a topic from one of 5 angles (**Far Past, Recent Past,
   Present, Trending, Manufactured**) and frame it with one of 7 proven headline formulas:
   **Recency** ("just happened"), **Relevancy** (ties to something the reader already cares
   about), **Celebrity** (a known name/brand), **Proximity** (close to home/their world),
   **Conflict** (two things in tension — "X says this, but actually..."), **Unusual** (breaks a
   pattern/expectation), **Ongoing** (a story already in motion, "part 2 of...").
2. **Retain — hold attention through curiosity**, via one of 3 devices: **Lists** (numbered,
   explicit count — "7 places your TAM is hiding"), **Steps** (ordered actions toward a goal),
   **Stories** (real events with a withheld lesson). This is why S1/S3's numbered-section
   structures work — they ARE the retain mechanism, not just formatting.
3. **Reward — actually deliver** on what the hook promised. A hook that doesn't pay off in the
   body is the single fastest way to burn trust — never open a gap you don't close.

**For us:** the **Conflict** formula (a trusted number/tool vs. the real truth) and **Unusual**
(the number goes the opposite direction from what people assume) are the two most reusable
angles for cold-email/data topics — most of our content is "here's what everyone assumes, here's
what's actually true."

## F§2 · MrBeast: the curiosity gap, weaponized

MrBeast writes the **title before making the video** — if the title doesn't create a gap, nothing
else matters. Grounded in George Loewenstein's information-gap theory (1994): curiosity is a felt
*deprivation* between what you know and what you want to know, and it peaks with a **small amount
of information, not zero and not too much**. Practical rules that transfer directly to a LinkedIn
hook:
- **State the gap, withhold the resolution.** Don't explain why yet — just make the gap
  undeniable ("Most of your TAM isn't where you're looking. Here's where it actually is.").
- **Specific and short beats clever.** MrBeast's titles are short and immediately state a
  concrete stake — no wordplay, no double meaning (same as Ogilvy's "avoid tricky headlines,"
  B§1 — both land on this independently, which makes it law per Part D).
- **Match the hook to the payoff.** He explicitly matches title/thumbnail to what happens in the
  first 5 seconds — the gap has to be immediately confirmed as real, or the reader bails.

## F§3 · The curiosity-gap test — run every hook against this before showing it

- [ ] **Is there an actual gap?** Does the hook state something is true/false/missing WITHOUT yet
  explaining why — leaving one specific, answerable question hanging?
- [ ] **Does it use Conflict or Unusual** (a trusted assumption vs. a specific contradicting
  fact), not just a flat statement of the topic?
- [ ] **Is the gap closeable in the body, immediately** — no bait-and-switch, the very next lines
  start resolving it?
- [ ] **Second person, specific, short** — "you," a real number, ≤130 characters (see the
  pre-publish checklist above).
- A hook that merely *describes* the topic ("here's a fact about X") has zero gap and reads flat.
  A hook that **contradicts an assumption the reader already holds** ("Apollo's number isn't your
  real TAM") forces the "wait, what?" reaction — that's the difference between informative and
  gripping.
